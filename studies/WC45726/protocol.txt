1.1            PROTOCOL SYNOPSIS
The purpose of this study is to assess the efficacy and safety of RO7795068, a dual glucagon-
like peptide-1 (GLP-1)/glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide (GIP) receptor agonist
(RA), as an adjunct to a reduced-calorie diet and increased physical activity in participants with
Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus (T2DM) who have obesity (body mass index [BMI]  30 kg/m2) or
overweight (BMI  27 and  30 kg/m2).
Obesity is a complex, chronic disease defined by excessive body fat, which increases the risk of
serious health complications like T2DM and cardiovascular disease. Achieving sustained
weight loss of over 10% can significantly improve health and even have disease-modifying
effects on related conditions. Current treatments involve long-term multidisciplinary
approaches, including lifestyle changes and pharmacotherapy.
While recent injectable incretin mimetics like semaglutide and tirzepatide have shown significant
efficacy, an unmet need remains for more therapeutic options with potentially better efficacy and
tolerability profiles. The molecule RO7795068 (CT-388) is a novel dual GLP-1/GIP receptor
agonist designed to address this need through a unique signaling mechanism expected to
deliver significant weight loss and improved glycemic control.

2.             INTRODUCTION
2.1            PURPOSE OF TRIAL
The purpose of this study is to assess the efficacy and safety of RO7795068, a dual
glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1)/glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide (GIP)
receptor agonist (RA) being developed for chronic weight management, as an adjunct to
a reduced-calorie diet and increased physical activity in participants with Type 2
Diabetes Mellitus (T2DM) who have obesity (body mass index [BMI]  30 kg/m2) or
overweight (BMI  27 and  30 kg/m2).

Obesity is a prevalent and complex chronic disease defined by the World Health
Organization as excessive adiposity that can impair health (WHO 2024). Obesity is
associated with an increased risk of complications including T2DM and cardiovascular
disease, imposing a substantive health and economic burden globally (Whitlock et al.
2009; Di Angelantonio et al. 2016; Tremmel et al. 2017).

The broad goals of obesity management extend beyond weight loss to improve patient
health, quality of life, and wellbeing, by preventing, improving, or resolving weight-related
complications (Yumuk et al. 2015; Busetto et al. 2024). Clinically meaningful weight loss
of 510% improves glycemic control, in patients who are overweight or obese with
T2DM reducing the need for glucose lowering medications (Ryan and Yockey 2017) and
improves other intermediate cardiovascular risk factors, for example, blood pressure and
lipids (Garvey et al. 2016; Ryan and Yockey 2017; Bray et al. 2018; ADA 2024).
Sustained weight loss of  10% can confer greater benefits, and disease-modifying
effects on several conditions, such as improving metabolic dysfunction-associated
steatohepatitis (MASH), obstructive sleep apnea, remission of T2DM, and long-term
cardiovascular outcomes and mortality (Yumuk et al. 2015; Garvey et al. 2016; Ryan
and Yockey 2017; Bray et al. 2018; Wharton et al. 2020; ADA 2024). In patients with
T2DM, weight management should represent a primary goal of treatment along with
glycemic management (ADA 2024).

As a chronic disease that often relapses, weight loss strategies to manage obesity
require long-term multidisciplinary approaches combining diet, exercise and other
behavioral interventions, pharmacotherapy, and in some cases, surgical procedures
(Jensen and Ryan 2014; Garvey et al. 2016; ADA 2024). Pharmacotherapy, in
conjunction with lifestyle modifications, is recommended for long-term weight loss in
individuals with obesity (BMI  30 kg/m2) or overweight (BMI  27 kg/m2) with
weight-related complications (Apovian et al. 2015; Yumuk et al. 2015; Garvey et al.
2016; Wharton et al. 2020; Cornier 2022; Grunvald et al. 2022; ADA 2024; Busetto et al.
2024).

Significant advancements in treatment options for chronic weight management in adults
living with overweight or obesity have been made in recent years with the approval of
several autoinjector-delivered incretin mimetics, particularly semaglutide and tirzepatide,

RO7795068—F. Hoffmann-La Roche Ltd
28/Protocol WC45726, Version 1
which have demonstrated weight loss of  10% and improved glycemic control, reaching
normoglycemia in adults with T2DM for a proportion of studied patients (Wilding et al.
2021; Jastreboff et al. 2022; Garvey et al. 2023). However, there remains a need for
more therapeutic options with potentially improved efficacy and tolerability profiles.

RO7795068 (otherwise known as CT-388) is a novel unimolecular dual GLP-1/GIP RA
peptide. Unlike endogenous incretins GLP-1 and GIP, RO7795068 is a biased agonist
that activates cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP) signaling but does not recruit
β-arrestin or cause Ca2+ release from either receptor. These signaling properties are
expected to be important for the clinical benefits, which include significant weight loss
and improved glycemic control.

Detailed information on RO7795068 is provided in the RO7795068 Investigator's
Brochure.

2.2           SUMMARY OF BENEFITS AND RISKS
Clinical evaluation of RO7795068 has been conducted in a completed Phase I study
CT-388-101 (XP45539), and is currently ongoing in two Phase II studies, CT-388-103
(XC45526) and CT-388-104 (XC45544).

CT-388-101 was a first-in-human randomized double-blind placebo-controlled study to
evaluate the safety, tolerability, PK, and PD of RO7795068 in otherwise healthy adult
participants with overweight and obesity and in patients with obesity and T2DM.The
study enrolled a total of 129 participants across multiple cohorts in single
ascending/multiple ascending/multiple dose (MD) parts. Data from the MD part of CT-
388-101 study demonstrated that once weekly (QW) SC administration of RO7795068 at
doses up to 22 mg achieved by up-titration had an acceptable tolerability and safety
profile and led to clinically meaningful body weight (BW) reduction and improved
glycemic control.

In participants with obesity without T2DM, treatment with 22 mg RO7795068 (n  24)
resulted in a placebo-adjusted least square mean (LSM) difference in percent change
BW from baseline of 18.8% (95% CI: 23.6%, 14.0%) at 24 weeks. The proportions
of participants achieving  5%,  10%,  15%, and  20% weight loss with RO7795068 at
Week 24 was 100%, 85%, 70%, and 45% respectively. Weight loss progressively
decreased over the treatment period, and no plateau had been reached at 24 weeks,
indicating that further decreases in body weight may be achievable with prolonged
treatment. In participants with obesity and T2DM, treatment with 22 mg RO7795068
(n  14) resulted in a placebo-adjusted LSM difference in percent change in BW from
baseline of 7.4% (95% CI: 9.8%, 4.9%) at Week 12 (compared to 11.5% in
non-T2DM participants at Week 12 in Cohort 12). These results are in line with data
from other incretin mimetic clinical studies and real world data for semaglutide and
tirzepatide which consistently demonstrate approximately 30% less weight loss in


RO7795068—F. Hoffmann-La Roche Ltd
29/Protocol WC45726, Version 1
participants with T2DM compared to participants without T2DM (Jastreboff et al. 2022;
Garvey et al. 2023; Rodriguez et al. 2025).

Clinically meaningful body weight loss was accompanied by similar patterns in reduction
from baseline in anthropometric parameters (BMI, waist circumference, hip
circumference and waist-to-height ratio) over time.

RO7795068 treatment also led to improvements in markers of glucose metabolism. A
consistent reduction in hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c) and fasting plasma glucose were
observed over time with RO7795068 treatment and was reflected by shifts in glycemic
status. For participants with obesity and T2DM, robust glycemic control including
normalizing of dysglycemia was observed within 612 weeks of RO7795068 treatment.
The placebo-adjusted LSM difference in absolute change form baseline in HbA1c at the
end of Week 12 was 2.85%, at which timepoint all T2DM participants (14/14) achieved
the HbA1c treatment goal of 6.5%, and 50% (7/14) reached normal HbA1c levels
(5.7%) (Steinberg et al. 2025).

A reduction in fasting glucose levels was observed within the first 2 weeks of
RO7795068 treatment and continued to improve thereafter. Compared to placebo,
participants with T2DM treated with RO7795068 for 6 weeks also experienced
meaningful reduction in self-monitored blood glucose (SMBG) profiles assessed at
multiple assessment points during the day, before and after meals. Glucose levels
generally remained within the optimal range of 70 mg/dL to 100 mg/dL at all timepoints
indicating effective glycemic control throughout the day.

Analyses of exploratory liver-related endpoints in obese participants without diabetes in
the CT-388-101 study showed that RO7795068 (22 mg) led to a robust decrease (59-
86%) in liver fat content measured by Magnetic Resonance Imaging-Proton Density Fat
Fraction (MRI-PDFF) at 24 weeks. The greatest effect was observed in participants with
severe metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease (MASLD).
Accompanying clinically meaningful improvements in cT1, a marker of liver fibrosis and
inflammation, and blood alanine aminotransferase (ALT) and ProC3 lab values, suggest
benefit in liver health (Steinberg et al. 2025).

In CT-388-101, multiple dosing of RO7795068 up to 22 mg achieved via dose up-
titration was shown to be safe with acceptable tolerability over a total treatment duration
of up to 24 weeks in otherwise healthy adult participants with obesity, and up to
12 weeks in participants with obesity and T2DM.

The safety profile of RO7795068 was generally consistent with that of approved incretin
mimetic GLP-1 RA and dual GLP-1/GIP RA drug classes (Sun et al 2015, Wegovy®
United States Prescribing Information (USPI), Wegovy® EU Summary of Product
Characteristics (SmPC), Zepbound® USPI, Mounjaro® EU SmPC). The predominant

In this study, the study drug will be delivered in a ready-to-use autoinjector (AI). The AI
is an integral drug-device combination product containing sterile solutions of RO7795068
of various concentrations or placebo in a pre-filled syringe (PFS) assembled into the AI.
The AI utilizes the Ypsomate® 1.0 mL AI platform. The YpsoMate 1.0 AI platform is a
well-established technology, and has been approved for use with other medicinal
products (e.g., Mazdutide); therefore, any additional risks associated with the use of the
AI is expected to be minimal (Ypsomate Product Brochure).

Taking into account the weight loss and improved glycemic control demonstrated in
clinical studies of participants with obesity/overweight with T2DM, the safety profile for
RO7795068, the AI, and the risk mitigation measures for the study, the benefitrisk ratio
is expected to be acceptable for RO7795068 in the setting of chronic weight
management.

4.              TRIAL DESIGN
4.1             DESCRIPTION OF TRIAL DESIGN
Study WC45726 is a Phase III, multi-center, randomized, double-blinded, placebo-
controlled, parallel group study to evaluate the efficacy and safety of RO7795068 at
8 mg, 16 mg, and 24 mg QW SC doses compared with placebo, when used in
conjunction with diet and exercise counseling for weight management, in participants
with T2DM who have obesity (BMI  30 kg/m2) or overweight (BMI  27 and  30 kg/m2).

A study schema is provided in Section 1.2 (see Figure 1). A schedule of activities and a
sample collection schedule are provided in Section 1.3.

Approximately 200 sites globally will participate to enroll approximately
1600 participants. Eligible patients will be randomized in a two-step process to one of
four treatment groups (RO7795068 8, 16, 24 mg, or placebo) to enroll an equal number
of participants (N  400/arm) per target dose arm and overall volume-matched placebo.
The randomization will be stratified by sex (male vs. female), prior GLP-1 RA-based
treatment (yes vs. no), HbA1c ( 8% vs.  8%), and baseline anti-hyperglycemic
medication (AHM) (weight gain promoting vs. weight loss promoting vs. weight neutral)
(see Table 4). A 20% enrollment cap on participants that have received prior GLP-1 RA-
based treatment will be implemented to ensure sufficient enrollment of GLP-1 RA-based
treatment naive participants. In addition, the study will have a 30% enrollment cap on
participants on sulfonylurea (SU) treatment at baseline to allow sufficient enrollment of
participants with other types of AHMs.

Prior GLP-1 RA-based treatment is defined as any use of any GLP-1 RA-based
treatment at any time prior to enrollment on Study WC45726. Examples of GLP-1 RA-
based treatments include, but are not limited to, dulaglutide, liraglutide, semaglutide, and
tirzepatide.

Study Periods
The study will include a screening period (4 weeks), a treatment period (72 weeks), and
a safety follow-up period (3 weeks). Participants who complete the treatment period will
have the opportunity to continue in a separate long-term extension (LTE) study.
RO7795068—F. Hoffmann-La Roche Ltd
39/Protocol WC45726, Version 1
During the screening period, participants will sign the Informed Consent Form (ICF) and
complete assessments to determine eligibility. Individuals who do not meet the eligibility
criteria for participation in this study (screen failure) may qualify for one re-screening
opportunity (refer to Section 5.6 for details). After eligibility is confirmed, the participants
will be randomized and receive their first dose of study drug on the same day or within
3 days after randomization.

The treatment period will include an up-titration period and a maintenance period.
During up-titration, participants will start at 4 mg with the goal to up-titrate the dose on a
monthly basis to achieve the assigned target maintenance dose (8, 16, or 24 mg; see
Figure 1). During the maintenance period, participants will continue on their assigned
target dose for the remainder of the treatment period. Dose modifications and/or
interruptions may be performed during the up-titration and/or maintenance period if
required (Section 12.4.2).

Participants who discontinue study treatment early will not be replaced. These
participants will return for a safety follow-up visit 4 weeks after their last dose and will
continue with study assessments up to and including the Week 72 study visit.
Participants who withdraw from the study will undergo an early discontinuation visit
within 7 days of their last dose, and for a safety follow-up visit 4 weeks after their last

4.2             RATIONALE FOR TRIAL DESIGN
4.2.1           Rationale for Trial Population
In Study WC45726, participants with T2DM who have obesity or overweight are eligible.
This population represents a clinically relevant population for pharmacotherapeutic
intervention for chronic weight management as they are at significant risk for disease
progression (e.g., development of cardiovascular disease, metabolic
dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease and MASH, and chronic kidney disease)
and consequently, are likely to benefit the most from weight loss (Khaodhiar et al. 2009).
Furthermore, to be considered eligible, participants must have previously tried and failed
diet/exercise weight loss intervention, which is consistent with current guidance that
stipulate lifestyle modification as first line treatment in chronic weight management (ADA
2024).

In the Phase I CT-388-101 study, RO7795068 was evaluated over 12 weeks in
participants with obesity without T2DM (Cohort 12) and obesity with T2DM (Cohort 13).
Participants with T2DM experienced a placebo-adjusted LSM weight loss of 7.38% at
Week 12, while those without T2DM showed a weight loss of 11.49% at Week 12. All
participants treated with 22 mg RO7795068 achieved over 5% weight loss by Week 12.
Therefore, CT-388-101 confirmed significant weight loss effects in both populations.
These results are in line with data from other GLP-1 RA-based therapy studies
(Jastreboff et al. 2022; Garvey et al. 2023) and real world data from semaglutide and
tirzepatide (Rodriguez et al. 2024), which demonstrated ~30% greater weight loss in
participants without T2DM compared with participants with T2DM. Because of the
anticipated differences in weight loss, given that participants with T2DM may have
specific safety concerns and concomitant medication use, and in line with regulatory
guidance (FDA 2025). Study WC45726 will specifically evaluate the efficacy and safety
of RO7795068 in participants with T2DM who have obesity or overweight.

4.2.2           Rationale for Trial Design
Study WC45726 is a multi-center, randomized, double-blinded, placebo-controlled,
parallel group study, designed to evaluate the efficacy and safety of RO7795068 across
different doses in adults with T2DM who have obesity or overweight. The double-blind
placebo-controlled design serves to minimize bias in the study data to rigorously assess
safety and efficacy in accordance with regulatory guidance (EMA 2016; FDA 2025).

In line with regulatory guidance and to ensure all participants, including participants
randomized to placebo, receive the standard of care for obesity management, the study
design incorporates counseling on diet and lifestyle modification according to current

RO7795068—F. Hoffmann-La Roche Ltd
42/Protocol WC45726, Version 1
guidelines for weight management (ADA 2025c) and T2DM management (ADA 2025d).
Management of T2DM with permitted antihyperglycemic medications (Section 6.10.2) is
allowed as long as these medications are at a stable dose for at least 3 months prior to
screening. To ensure participant safety, participants who develop significant persistent
hyperglycemia during the treatment period will be allowed to initiate rescue therapy
(Section 6.9.1).

The planned treatment duration is 72 weeks. This should allow for a minimum 52-week
treatment duration at the target maintenance dose after the up-titration period, which is
considered necessary to be able to robustly assess the benefitrisk profile of each dose
compared to placebo and is consistent with regulatory guidelines (EMA 2016; FDA
2025).

4.2.3          Rationale for Choice of Stratification Factors
At the time of randomization, participants will be stratified by sex (male vs. female), prior
GLP-1 RA -based treatment (yes vs. no), HbA1c ( 8% vs.  8%), and baseline AHM
(weight gain promoting vs. weight loss promoting vs. weight neutral). These stratification
factors were selected to ensure balanced distribution of key patient characteristics
across treatment arms, as they may influence treatment efficacy and safety outcomes.

A large body of evidence suggests that females may experience greater weight
reduction compared to males when treated with GLP-1 RAs. For example, one
meta-analysis encompassing 14 studies demonstrated that females lost, on average,
1.04 kg more than males (95% CI: 0.701.38), which corresponded to a 1.69% greater
percentage weight loss (95% CI: 0.782.61) (Yang et al. 2025). The observed
differential efficacy is likely rooted in underlying physiological, metabolic, and hormonal
distinctions between sexes.

Based on clinical trial data and real-world evidence, there is growing evidence that prior
GLP-1 RA use could be a significant confounder in assessing the efficacy of a weight
loss treatment. Evidence from both clinical trials and real-world data suggests that
people with prior exposure to a GLP-1 RA drug may have a blunted response to
subsequent treatment. Studies like STEP-1 (Wilding et al. 2022) and SURMOUNT-4
(Aronne et al. 2024) show that some treatment benefits (i.e., weight loss,
cardiometabolic markers, lipids, A1c, etc.) are sustained even a year after stopping a
GLP-1 RA drug. Consequently, these individuals may respond differently than those that
have never been treated with a GLP-1 RA drug. Real-world data indicates that the initial
response to a new GLP-1 RA may be less pronounced in those with previous use. For
instance, one study found that prior GLP-1 RA users initiating tirzepatide had less weight
loss (up to 3.5 kg less) and a smaller A1c reduction (up to 0.7% less) compared to GLP-
1 RA naive patients (Buckley et al. 2024).

The diverse effects of different classes of AHM on bodyweight are well established. For
example, SU are commonly associated with weight gain, metformin is generally
RO7795068—F. Hoffmann-La Roche Ltd
43/Protocol WC45726, Version 1
considered weight neutral and sodium-glucose cotransporter-2 (SGLT-2) inhibitors are
associated with weight loss (ADA 2025d). Lastly, baseline HbA1c levels are a significant
prognostic factor influencing not only the magnitude of glycemic improvement, but also
the response to weight loss interventions (FDA 2023; Garvey et al. 2024). Stratifying by
baseline AHM and HbA1c is also aligned with FDA guidelines for chronic weight
management trials that involve participants with T2DM (FDA 2025).

4.2.4         Justification for Dose and Schedule
The proposed dosing strategy with a starting dose of 4 mg and target maintenance
doses of 8 mg, 16 mg, and 24 mg, and titration scheme are comprehensively supported
by a robust body of evidence gathered from completed Phase I (CT-388-101) and
ongoing Phase II (CT-388-103, CT-388-104) studies, alongside population
pharmacokinetic/pharmacodynamic (PK/PD) modeling and simulation. Data from these
studies, including interim analyses, have demonstrated that RO7795068 promotes
robust weight loss and improvement in glycemic control while maintaining acceptable
safety and tolerability profiles across various patient populations, including those with
and without T2DM.

4.2.5         Rationale for Collection of Information on Race and Ethnicity
Data pertaining to participant race and ethnicity represents a component of the broad
demographic profile of the study population. Collection of demographic data, including
information on race and ethnicity, where permitted, is of importance to the future
interpretation of results from the clinical trial, including identification of potential
differences in efficacy, safety, and pharmacokinetics among participants. Collection of
race and ethnicity data may enable investigation of potential relationships between
biomarkers and race or ethnicity, including determination of whether race or ethnicity
could be a prognostic factor. Collection of these data may also contribute to a better
understanding of the distribution of overweight and obesity according to race or ethnicity.

4.2.6         Rationale for Biomarker Assessments
Obesity is a heterogeneous disease, and across different weight loss interventions,
patients vary in their degree of weight loss and improvements in obesity-related
comorbidities. Therefore, all participants may not be equally likely to benefit from
treatment with RO7795068. Biomarker assessments in plasma, serum, and urine
collected before and at various timepoints after treatment, will be used to provide
evidence of the biologic activity of RO7795068 in participants, identify biomarkers that
may be predictive of treatment response to RO7795068, define PK and
pharmacodynamic (PD) relationships, advance the understanding of the mechanism of
action of RO7795068 in participants, support selection of a recommended dose regimen,
and increase the knowledge and understanding of disease biology. Exploratory
biomarkers may include, but will not be limited to, markers of glucose homeostasis (e.g.,
C-peptide), fibro-inflammation (e.g., PRO-C3, IL-6), and other pathophysiology
biomarkers of obesity-related comorbidities (e.g., NT-ProBNP, urinary 8-isoprostane).

4.2.7          Rationale for MRI Substudy
A thorough understanding of the effects of dual GLP-1/GIP RAs, such as RO7795068,
on lean mass quality and quantity accompanying significant weight loss is necessary to
better understand the quality of weight changes associated with therapy (EMA 2016;
FDA 2025). Additionally, changes in hepatic and pancreatic adipose tissue in obese
populations induced by these agents are not fully explored. These MRI assessments are
designed to provide direct evidence of RO7795068's impact on overall body
composition, including detailed lean mass assessment and ectopic adipose depots.
These insights, extending beyond total body weight change, are important for a more
complete evaluation of study drug efficacy and safety, and its potential to influence
underlying disease biology. Understanding these nuanced effects on tissue composition
will contribute significantly to the characterization of RO7795068 and the mechanisms
contributing to its potential metabolic benefits.

5.5.1          Diet
Dietary counseling will consist of advice regarding healthy food choices and focus on
calorie restriction using a hypocaloric diet with a recommended macronutrient
composition of the following:
     Maximum 25% of energy from fat
     Approximately 25% of energy from protein
     Approximately 50% of energy from carbohydrates

estimated total energy expenditure is recommended. Participants with significant
suppression of appetite must be counseled to consume at least 900 kcal/day, with the
emphasis on an adequate protein intake. The hypocaloric diet and physical activity
should continue throughout the treatment period. If a BMI  22 kg/m2 is reached, the
recommended energy intake should be recalculated with no kcal deficit for the remainder
of the study. This guidance should be considered as a recommendation and could be
altered per the participant’s needs. Refer to Section 12.4.2.2 for guidance on dose
modifications if a BMI  22 kg/m2 reached.

Regular physical activity (150 minutes of moderate intensity physical activity per week
[e.g., brisk walking]) will be encouraged during the study.

participants on symptoms and management of hyperglycemia and hypoglycemia, SMBG

        The condition that leads to the procedure is the adverse event. A therapeutic or

Investigators will seek information on adverse events during each contact with the

10.              STATISTICAL CONSIDERATIONS
The primary analysis will be performed when all participants have completed the Week
72 visit or have otherwise discontinued from the study, whichever occurs first.

10.1             STATISTICAL HYPOTHESES
The purpose of the study is to assess the superiority of RO7795068 (each of Arm 2, 3
and Arm 4) compared with placebo (Arm 1) in terms of the primary endpoints in both
overall and GLP-1 RA-based treatment naive populations. The hypothesis for the
primary analysis will be assessed in the Efficacy Analysis Set - Overall (EAS-O) and
EAS- Treatment Naïve (EAS-N; see Section 10.2). The two-sided null and alternative
hypotheses for the primary estimands can be expressed as:

      H0: ΔArm 2  ΔArm 1 and HA: ΔArm 2 ≠ ΔArm 1

RO7795068—F. Hoffmann-La Roche Ltd
91/Protocol WC45726, Version 1
    H0: ΔArm 3  ΔArm 1 and HA: ΔArm 3≠ΔArm 1
    H0: ΔArm 4  ΔArm 1 and HA: ΔArm 4 ≠ ΔArm 1

Where ΔArm X and ΔArm 1 are the mean percent (%) change from baseline in body weight
at Week 72 in Arm X and Arm 1 respectively.

Null and alternative hypotheses of a similar form will be tested for all key secondary
efficacy analyses. All hypothesis tests will be two-sided unless stated otherwise.

A prespecified graphical scheme (Bretz et al. 2009, 2011) will be implemented to control
the family-wise error rate at a 2-sided alpha level of 0.05 for testing the hypothesis for
the primary and key secondary endpoints across arms and populations. This graphical
approach is a closed testing procedure and strongly controls the family-wise error rate.
The testing scheme will be fully detailed in the SAP. For each arm, hypothesis testing for
the primary endpoints on RO7795068 against placebo will be conducted hierarchically in
the order of EAS - Treatment Naïve followed by EAS – Overall. No multiplicity
adjustment is planned between treatment policy and hypothetical estimands.

10.1.1         Sample Size Determination
A sample size of 1600 (400 per arm) participants will provide  90% power to
demonstrate superiority of RO7795068 for each of Arm 3 and Arm 4 compared to
placebo (Arm 1) for the primary endpoint of percent (%) change from baseline in body
weight at Week 72 in overall population under the following assumptions:
   1:1:1:1 randomization ratio (Arm 1: Arm 2: Arm 3: Arm 4)
   Delta of at least 7% and a common standard deviation (SD) of 10%
   Two sample t-tests with equal variance
   Two‑sided type I error rate of 0.05/3
   20%, and 30% dropout rate by Week 72 for experimental arm, and control arm,
    respectively
Assuming 80% of enrolled participants are GLP-1 RA-based treatment naïve patients. A
sample size of 1280 (320 per arm) participants will provide  90% power to demonstrate
superiority of RO7795068 for each of Arm 3 and Arm 4 compared to placebo (Arm 1) for
the primary endpoint of percent (%) change from baseline in body weight at Week 72 in
treatment naïve population assuming delta of at least 8%, and the same assumptions
above on randomization ratio, SD, test, type I error and dropout.

10.3.1         General Considerations
The primary analysis will occur after all participants have completed Week 72 visit or
have discontinued from the study, whichever occurs first.

Descriptive summaries/statistics will include number of participants, mean, median,
standard deviation, minimum, and maximum for continuous variables and count and
proportion for categorical variables.

Unless specified otherwise: Efficacy analyses will be performed on the EAS-O and/or
EAS-N. Safety analyses will be performed on the SAS-R and/or SAS-T. All tests for
treatment effects will be performed using a 2-sided alpha level of 0.05, with 2-sided
95% CIs provided. Baseline is defined as the last available non-missing measurement
prior to the first dose of study treatment.

10.3.2         Estimation Methods for the Primary Estimands
The primary objective will be assessed separately according to the treatment policy
estimand and hypothetical estimand (See Section 3.1).

Treatment Policy Estimand
The analysis for the primary endpoint according to the treatment policy estimand will be
performed in the EAS-O and/or EAS-N using DPS-T (see Section 10.2) using an
analysis of covariance (ANCOVA) model with percent change in body weight from
baseline at Week 72 as the dependent variable and treatment group, baseline body
weight and stratification factors as independent variables. Full model specification will be
provided in SAP.

Observed data after the ICE are used for analysis. Missing data after ICE will be imputed
using retrieved dropout (or reference-based method if not enough retrieved dropouts).
Missing data not after ICE will be imputed assuming missing at random (MAR).

Hypothetical Estimand
The analysis of the primary endpoint according to the hypothetical estimand will be
performed in the EAS-O and/or EAS-N using DPS-H (see Section 10.2), using a mixed
model for repeated measures (MMRM). Percent change from baseline in body weight at
each schedule assessment (visit) will be included in the model as dependent variable.
Treatment group, visit, baseline body weight, stratification factors, treatment-by-visit
RO7795068—F. Hoffmann-La Roche Ltd
94/Protocol WC45726, Version 1
interaction, baseline body weight-by-visit interaction and stratification factors-by-visit
interaction will be included as fixed effects. The MMRM model will assume an
unstructured covariance structure. If there are convergence problems with the model,
the following covariance structures together with a sandwich estimator will be
implemented in order until convergence is achieved: first-order ante-dependence,
heterogeneous Toeplitz, and AR(1). Full model specification will be provided in SAP.

Observed data will be censored at the ICE. Missing data and post-ICE data will be
imputed implicitly using MMRM assuming missing at random (MAR).

For treatment policy and hypothetical estimand separately, RO7795068 will be declared
superior to placebo in percent weight change at Week 72 if the p-value is less than the
alpha level allocated to the hypothesis according to the graphical approach (to be fully
specified in the SAP).

12.4.1        Risks Associated with RO7795068
RO7795068 is not approved by health authorities, and clinical development is ongoing.
The safety plan for participants in this study is based on clinical experience with
RO7795068 in completed and ongoing studies. The anticipated important safety risks
for RO7795068 are outlined below. Please refer to the RO7795068 Investigator's
Brochure for a complete summary of safety information.

Guidelines for management of AEs are provided in Table 14.

12.4.1.1      Severe GI-related Adverse Events
Gastrointestinal (GI) adverse reactions (e.g., nausea, vomiting and diarrhea), sometimes
severe, have been reported with the GLP-1 RA-based drug class (Sun et al. 2015;
Wegovy USPI and Wegovy® SmPC; Zepbound USPI; Mounjaro SmPC). GLP-1 RA-
based drugs slow gastric emptying by activating GLP-1 receptors in the stomach and
intestines. This delay in gastric emptying helps to reduce postprandial blood glucose
levels by slowing the absorption of nutrients. However, delayed gastric emptying can
lead to nausea, bloating, and early satiety, as food remains in the stomach longer than
usual. This is often experienced as GI discomfort and is one of the main reasons for
nausea and vomiting in participants.

12.4.1.2      Acute Gallbladder or Biliary Disease
Acute gallbladder disease such as cholelithiasis or cholecystitis has been reported with
the GLP-1 RA-based drug class. Rapid weight loss through the use of GLP-1 RA-based
therapy can sometimes precipitate gall stone formation.

12.4.1.3      Acute Pancreatitis
Acute pancreatitis has been reported with the GLP-1 RA-based drug class. A sudden
onset of inflammation of the pancreas can be caused by gallstones, severe
hypertriglyceridemia and some medications.

12.4.1.4      Hypoglycemia
Hypoglycemia can occur naturally throughout the day due to factors such as fasting,
physical activity, and hormonal fluctuations. For patients with and without T2DM, blood
glucose levels fluctuate in response to meals, insulin, and other hormonal actions
(e.g., cortisol, glucagon). Hypoglycemia (blood glucose  3.9 mmol/L [ 70 mg/dL]) has
been reported in patients with or without T2DM treated with GLP-1 RA-based therapy.

Upon signing the ICF, all participants will be educated about signs and symptoms of
hypoglycemia, how to treat hypoglycemia, and how to collect appropriate information for
each episode of hypoglycemia.




RO7795068—F. Hoffmann-La Roche Ltd
107/Protocol WC45726, Version 1
Hypoglycemia may be identified by spontaneous reporting of symptoms from
participants (whether confirmed or unconfirmed by simultaneous glucose values) or by
blood glucose samples collected during study visits.

Events of hypoglycemia should only be reported as an AE if considered clinically
significant by the investigator.

To avoid duplicate reporting, all consecutive blood glucose values  70 mg/dL
(3.9 mmol/L) occurring within a 1-hour period may be considered to be a single
hypoglycemic event (Weinberg et al. 2010).

Appropriate monitoring and follow-up of hypoglycemia of any severity must be
conducted and an unscheduled visit should be performed within 7 days of the
hypoglycemic event(s) to evaluate participant status and glycemic status.

12.4.1.5       Medullary Thyroid Cancer
In carcinogenicity studies in mice and rats, the GLP-1 RA semaglutide caused a dose-
dependent and treatment duration-dependent increase in the incidence of thyroid C-cell

Postponing Scheduled Up-Titration
Postponing scheduled up-titration and continued administration at the current dose may

investigator to address safety or tolerability issues. All participants must attempt one or
more up-titrations to the next higher dose level at subsequent study visits, if considered
safe to do so per investigator judgment.

Dose Reduction
If the investigator decides that the clinical situation warrants a dose reduction, the
participant’s dose may be reduced by 1 dose level at a time. All participants must
attempt one or more up-titrations to the next higher dose level at subsequent study
visits, if considered safe to do so per investigator judgment. Participants must receive a
minimum of 2 doses of the lower dose before attempting up-titration to the next dose,
and all up-titrations must occur at a scheduled in-person study visit. If up-titration to the
next dose is not tolerated, the participant should remain on the highest tolerated dose
and further up-titration challenges are per PI judgment. If the participant continues to
have tolerability or safety issues, the study drug should be temporarily interrupted (see
Section 12.4.2.3).

12.4.2.3       Treatment Interruption
In rare situations, a temporary treatment interruption of the study drug may be needed to
address tolerability or safety issues, or unexpected medical events. The rationale for
treatment interruption should be documented in the eCRF. Treatment interruptions for
other reasons (i.e., not related to the safety of the participant) are not permitted.

If treatment is interrupted (regardless of the reason), participants should be reassessed
for suitability to restart study drug in weekly intervals following the initial missed dose.
The below guidance outlines the dose at which study drug should be resumed after
interruption, which will be pre-specified by IxRS:
   1 missed dose: Restart at the same dose level achieved prior to treatment
    interruption and continue per planned Schedule of Activities
   2 consecutive missed doses: Restart at one dose level lower than dose level
    achieved prior to treatment interruption, or restart at same dose per PI judgment
   3 or 4 consecutive missed doses: Restart at two dose levels lower than dose
    level achieved prior to treatment interruption or lowest dose, whichever is higher
    5 consecutive missed doses: Restart up-titration from lowest dose

These guidelines are intended as a reference and should not supersede pertinent local
or institutional SOPs.

Procedures
In the event of a suspected anaphylactic reaction during study treatment administration,
the following procedures should be performed:
1. Stop the study treatment administration, if possible.
2. Call for additional medical assistance.
3. Maintain an adequate airway.
4. Ensure that appropriate monitoring is in place, with continuous ECG and pulse
   oximetry monitoring if possible.
5. Administer antihistamines, epinephrine, or other medications and intravenous (IV)
   fluids as required by participant status and as directed by the physician in charge.
6. Continue to observe the participant and document observations.
7. Collect serum samples for immunogenicity testing.
8. Ask the participant to return for immunogenicity sample collection at the time of
   washout, if appropriate.

Required Equipment and Medication
The following equipment and medication are needed in the event of a suspected
anaphylactic reaction during study treatment administration in a clinical setting:
   Monitoring devices: ECG monitor, BP monitor, oxygen saturation monitor, and
    thermometer
   Oxygen
   Epinephrine for intramuscular (preferred route), subcutaneous, intravenous,
    or endotracheal administration in accordance with institutional guidelines
   Antihistamines
   Corticosteroids
   IV infusion solutions, tubing, catheters, and tape

12.8.1        Overview of the Research Biosample Repository
The RBR is a centrally administered group of facilities used for the long-term storage of
human biological specimens, including body fluids, solid tissues, and derivatives thereof
(e.g., DNA, RNA, proteins, peptides). The collection, storage, and analysis of RBR
samples will facilitate the rational design of new pharmaceutical agents and the

RO7795068—F. Hoffmann-La Roche Ltd
143/Protocol WC45726, Version 1
development of diagnostic tests, which may allow for individualized drug therapy for
patients in the future.

Samples for the RBR will be collected from participants who give specific consent to
participate in this optional research. RBR samples will be analyzed to achieve one or
more of the following objectives:
   To study the association of biomarkers with efficacy or disease progression
   To identify safety biomarkers that are associated with susceptibility to developing
    adverse events or can lead to improved adverse event monitoring or investigation
   To increase knowledge and understanding of disease biology and drug safety
   To study drug response, including drug effects and the processes of drug absorption
    and disposition
   To develop biomarker or diagnostic assays and establish the performance
    characteristics of these assays

12.8.2         Approval by the Institutional Review Board or Ethics
               Committee
Collection, storage, and analysis of RBR samples is contingent upon the review and
approval of the exploratory research portion and the RBR portion of the Informed
Consent Form by each site's IRB/EC and, if applicable, an appropriate regulatory body.
If a site has not been granted approval for RBR sampling, this section of the protocol
(Section 12.8) will not be applicable at that site.

provide optional RBR samples. Participants who choose not to provide optional RBR
samples will not provide a separate signature. The investigator should document
whether or not the participant has given consent to provide optional RBR samples and (if
applicable) the date of consent, by completing the Sample Informed Consent/Withdrawal
eCRF.

In the event of an RBR participant's death or loss of competence, the participant's
samples and data will continue to be used as part of the RBR research.

12.8.6        Withdrawal from the Research Biosample Repository
Participants who give consent to provide RBR samples have the right to withdraw their
consent at any time for any reason. After withdrawal of consent, any remaining samples
(i.e., all biological material) will be destroyed. However, if RBR samples have been
tested prior to withdrawal of consent, results from those tests will remain as part of the
overall research data. If a participant wishes to withdraw consent to the testing of his or
her RBR samples during the study, the investigator must inform the Medical Monitor in
writing of the participant's wishes through use of the appropriate RBR Subject
Withdrawal Form and must enter the date of withdrawal on the Sample Informed
Consent/Withdrawal eCRF. If a participant wishes to withdraw consent to the testing of
his or her RBR samples after closure of the site, the investigator must inform the
Sponsor by emailing the study number and participant number to the following
email address: global.rcr-withdrawal@roche.com
A participant's withdrawal from this study does not, by itself, constitute withdrawal of
consent for testing of RBR samples. Likewise, a participant's withdrawal of consent for
testing of RBR samples does not constitute withdrawal from this study.
